---
title: SPRINT — intensive vs standard blood-pressure control (NEJM 2015)
url: https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1511939
type: study
---
SPRINT randomized about 9,300 adults at high cardiovascular risk (but without diabetes or prior stroke) to an intensive systolic blood-pressure target below 120 mm Hg versus a standard target below 140 mm Hg. The intensive arm reduced major cardiovascular events by roughly 25% and all-cause mortality by roughly 27%, and the trial was stopped early for benefit after a median of about 3.3 years.

The crucial teaching caveat is measurement technique. SPRINT used a protocolized, often unattended, automated office blood-pressure measurement, which tends to read several mm Hg lower than a routine clinic cuff. A "120" in SPRINT is therefore not the same number as a hurried manual clinic reading, so the target cannot be applied naively to casually measured pressures.

There were trade-offs: the intensive group had more hypotension, syncope, electrolyte abnormalities, and acute kidney injury. The generalizable principle is that a trial's benefit is inseparable from how its key variable was measured and which population was studied (SPRINT excluded diabetics and stroke patients), so applying the result requires reproducing the measurement method and respecting the enrollment criteria.
