---
title: RALES — spironolactone in severe heart failure (NEJM 1999)
url: https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199909023411001
type: study
---
RALES randomized patients with severe heart failure (NYHA class III to IV) and a reduced ejection fraction (35% or less), already treated with an ACE inhibitor and a loop diuretic, to low-dose spironolactone (a mineralocorticoid-receptor antagonist) versus placebo. Spironolactone reduced all-cause mortality by about 30% (roughly 35% versus 46%), reducing both pump-failure deaths and sudden cardiac death, and the trial was stopped early for benefit.

RALES helped establish mineralocorticoid-receptor antagonists as a pillar of guideline-directed therapy for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF).

The most important teaching point is the gap between a trial and real-world implementation. RALES used a low dose (commonly 25 mg) with protocolized potassium and renal monitoring and excluded patients with significant baseline renal dysfunction or hyperkalemia. After publication, population studies showed rising hospitalizations and deaths from hyperkalemia when the drug was prescribed more broadly and less carefully. The generalizable lesson: a mortality benefit depends on appropriate patient selection and on the monitoring that made the trial safe, not on the pill alone.
