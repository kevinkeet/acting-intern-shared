Two efficient, evidence-supported frameworks structure clinical teaching at the point of care.

The One-Minute Preceptor (OMP) is a five-microskill model used by the teacher: (1) get a commitment ("what do you think is going on?"), (2) probe for supporting reasoning ("what led you there?"), (3) teach a general rule that transfers beyond this case, (4) reinforce what was done well, and (5) correct mistakes constructively. It externalizes the learner's reasoning before the teacher supplies an answer, which is why it is more instructive than simply giving the diagnosis.

SNAPPS is a learner-driven counterpart for case presentations: Summarize the case, Narrow the differential to two or three, Analyze the differential by comparing and contrasting, Probe the preceptor with questions about uncertainties, Plan management, and Select an issue for self-directed learning. SNAPPS shifts cognitive work to the learner and surfaces their reasoning gaps.

Teaching point for an AI tutor: good teaching does not just deliver the answer; it asks for a commitment, makes the underlying rule explicit, and ends with a probing question that the learner must work through. The "Check yourself" prompt in these teaching points is the OMP "get a commitment" step applied in reverse.
