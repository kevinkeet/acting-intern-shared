title: ARDSNet (ARMA) — low tidal volume ventilation in ARDS (NEJM 2000)

The ARDSNet ARMA trial established lung-protective ventilation as standard of care in acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). Patients were randomized to a lower tidal volume strategy (about 6 mL/kg of predicted body weight, with a plateau pressure limit around 30 cm H2O) versus the then-conventional 12 mL/kg.

The trial was stopped early: lower tidal volumes reduced in-hospital mortality by roughly 9 percentage points (about 31% versus 40%) and increased ventilator-free days. The mechanism is reduction of ventilator-induced lung injury from overdistension (volutrauma) and repeated alveolar opening and closing.

Two teaching points follow. First, dose ventilation to PREDICTED body weight (a function of height and sex), not actual weight, because lung size scales with height; using actual weight systematically over-ventilates. Second, this is a counterintuitive result for trainees who equate larger breaths with better gas exchange: accepting permissive hypercapnia and smaller volumes improves survival. ARMA is the empirical foundation for the 6 mL/kg target used in modern ARDS bundles.
