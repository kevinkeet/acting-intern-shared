Workup for suspected pulmonary embolism (PE) should begin with a structured pretest probability, not an immediate scan. Two validated tools anchor this. The Wells score (Philip Wells, 1990s) stratifies patients into low, moderate, or high probability using items such as clinical signs of DVT, PE being the most likely diagnosis, tachycardia, immobilization or recent surgery, prior VTE, hemoptysis, and active malignancy.

The Pulmonary Embolism Rule-out Criteria (PERC), derived by Jeff Kline and colleagues, is a set of eight clinical features (including age under 50, heart rate under 100, oxygen saturation at or above 95%, no hemoptysis, no estrogen use, no prior VTE, no recent surgery or trauma, and no unilateral leg swelling). In a patient already judged low risk, satisfying all eight PERC criteria lowers the probability of PE below the testing threshold, so no D-dimer or imaging is needed.

The teaching point is sequencing: use Wells (or a similar rule) to set the pretest probability, apply PERC only in low-risk patients to avoid unnecessary D-dimer, and reserve CT pulmonary angiography for those who cannot be ruled out clinically. This prevents both missed PE and overtesting.
