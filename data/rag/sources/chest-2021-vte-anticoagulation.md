title: CHEST 2021 — antithrombotic therapy for VTE disease

The 2021 CHEST guideline on antithrombotic therapy for venous thromboembolism (VTE) sets the modern framework for how long and with what agent to anticoagulate. For most acute DVT or PE without contraindication, a minimum treatment phase of three months is strongly recommended. A direct oral anticoagulant (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, or dabigatran) is suggested over a vitamin K antagonist for most patients.

Duration beyond three months turns on WHY the clot happened. VTE provoked by a major transient risk factor (for example major surgery) should generally stop at three months, because the recurrence risk falls once the provocation resolves. Unprovoked VTE, or VTE with a persistent risk factor such as active cancer, carries a high ongoing recurrence risk and favors extended anticoagulation, with periodic reassessment of the bleeding-versus-clotting balance.

For cancer-associated VTE, an oral factor Xa inhibitor is now generally preferred over low-molecular-weight heparin for many patients. The teaching framework is the provoked-versus-unprovoked distinction as the hinge for duration, and an explicit, recurring weighing of thrombotic risk against bleeding risk rather than a fixed stop date.
