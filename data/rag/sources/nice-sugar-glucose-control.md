---
title: NICE-SUGAR — intensive vs conventional glucose control in the ICU (NEJM 2009)
url: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19318384/
type: study
---
NICE-SUGAR randomized about 6,100 critically ill adults to intensive glucose control (target 81 to 108 mg/dL) versus conventional control (target 180 mg/dL or less). Contrary to earlier single-center enthusiasm for tight glycemic control, the intensive arm had HIGHER 90-day mortality (about 27.5% versus 25%) and far more severe hypoglycemia.

The result reversed practice: ICU glucose is now generally targeted to roughly 140 to 180 mg/dL rather than tight normalization. The likely harm is from iatrogenic hypoglycemia and glycemic variability outweighing any benefit of normoglycemia.

The teaching point is a reasoning trap: a physiologically appealing target (normal blood glucose) is not the same as a better patient outcome, and a striking early single-center trial can be overturned by a large multicenter trial. NICE-SUGAR is a clean example of why practice should rest on adequately powered, generalizable evidence and why "more normal numbers" can cause net harm.
