## Q1 — Antibiotics in suspected sepsis
**Key trials** — [Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8486643/): risk-stratified antibiotic urgency — immediate for shock/high probability, a brief investigatory window (~3h) when uncertain and no shock. Rests on guideline consensus and observational data, not a clean RCT of timing.
**Teaching points**
- Persistent hypotension or lactate ≥4 after fluids = shock → antibiotics within 1 hour, no delay for cultures.
- Cultures before antibiotics, but never let collection delay therapy in shock.
- Empiric choice is source/host-driven: vanco + antipseudomonal β-lactam for undifferentiated/high MRSA risk; anaerobic cover for intra-abdominal; antifungals only with candidemia risk.
- Classic error: reflexive broad-spectrum for every "possible" infection without shock — resistance, AKI, C. diff. De-escalate at 48–72h.
**Clinical reasoning** — Pretest-probability/competing-risk trade-off: weigh harm of delayed therapy against overtreatment, calibrating to sepsis likelihood + shock ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q2 — Workup of new microcytic anemia
**Key trials** — No single trial; hematology guideline consensus. Ferritin <15–30 ng/mL essentially diagnostic; AGA/BSG mandate bidirectional endoscopy in men and postmenopausal women with confirmed IDA ([BSG iron deficiency guideline](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=BSG+guideline+management+iron+deficiency+anaemia)).
**Teaching points**
- Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant — falsely normal/high in inflammation, liver disease, malignancy. Borderline 30–100 + suspicion → add TSAT (<20%) and CRP.
- Mentzer index (MCV/RBC): >13 iron deficiency, <13 thalassemia trait. High RDW favors iron deficiency.
- Classic error: stopping at "iron deficiency" without finding the source — it's a sign, not a diagnosis.
- Don't transfuse or scope a thalassemia trait mistaken for refractory IDA.
**Clinical reasoning** — Diagnostic schema, first split on ferritin/iron stores; then resist premature closure — the second step is "why is iron low?" ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q3 — Anticoagulating atrial fibrillation
**Key trials** — [ARISTOTLE (2011)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ARISTOTLE+apixaban+atrial+fibrillation): apixaban beat warfarin on stroke, bleeding, mortality. [RE-LY (2009)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=RE-LY+dabigatran+atrial+fibrillation): dabigatran 150 reduced stroke. [RE-ALIGN (2013)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=RE-ALIGN+dabigatran+mechanical+valve): dabigatran worse with mechanical valves.
**Teaching points**
- AF pattern is irrelevant — paroxysmal = persistent for stroke risk; score the same.
- Mechanical valve and moderate-severe MS are the only true "DOAC contraindicated" valve lesions; bioprosthetics and most others can take a DOAC.
- Classic error: substituting aspirin, or withholding on a high HAS-BLED — instead correct modifiable bleeding factors.
- Sex is a risk *modifier*, not an independent driver.
**Clinical reasoning** — Competing-risk trade-off with a validated pretest tool: CHA₂DS₂-VASc vs modifiable bleeding risk ([MDCalc](https://www.mdcalc.com)).

## Q4 — Oxygen target in COPD exacerbation
**Key trials** — [BTS/NICE emergency oxygen](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=BTS+guideline+emergency+oxygen+use): 88–92% in COPD/at-risk-of-hypercapnia. [Austin (2010) prehospital titrated O₂](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=Austin+oxygen+COPD+prehospital+titrated+mortality): high-flow O₂ ~doubled mortality vs titrated.
**Teaching points**
- Target 88–92% via 24–28% Venturi for predictable FiO₂, not open cannula chasing 100%.
- Harm is hyperoxia-driven CO₂ retention (V/Q, Haldane), not hypoxia per se — but never let SpO₂ drop in a peri-arrest patient.
- pH <7.35 + hypercapnia despite controlled O₂ = trigger for NIV, not a reason to cut oxygen.
- Classic error: pulling oxygen off a desaturating retainer "to protect drive" — titrate down, don't withhold.
**Clinical reasoning** — Competing-risk trade-off: immediate hypoxia vs slower hypercapnic acidosis → an explicit saturation band ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q5 — tPA in acute ischemic stroke
**Key trials** — [NINDS (1995)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=NINDS+tPA+acute+ischemic+stroke): tPA within 3h improved 90-day outcomes despite more sICH. [ECASS III (2008)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ECASS+III+alteplase+3+to+4.5+hours): extended to 3–4.5h. [WAKE-UP (2018)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=WAKE-UP+trial+DWI+FLAIR+mismatch): MRI mismatch guides unknown-onset.
**Teaching points**
- Time is from last-known-well, not symptom discovery.
- BP <185/110 before bolus, <180/105 for 24h after; treat first, don't withhold for a transient reading.
- tPA and thrombectomy are not either/or — don't let one delay the other.
- Classic error: withholding lysis for "minor" but disabling deficits (aphasia, hemianopia) — disability, not NIHSS number, drives eligibility.
**Clinical reasoning** — Competing-risk under time pressure: disability-free benefit vs sICH, ratio decaying every minute; use a structured checklist to avoid omission errors ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q6 — Distinguishing SIADH
**Key trials** — No landmark RCT; consensus criteria. European hyponatremia guideline (2014) + Schwartz-Bartter criteria ([European hyponatremia guideline (2014)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=European+clinical+practice+guideline+hyponatremia+diagnosis+treatment)).
**Teaching points**
- Confirm hypotonicity first (serum osm <275); skipping misses pseudohyponatremia and hyperglycemia (correct Na ~2.4 per 100 mg/dL glucose).
- Classic error: calling SIADH without morning cortisol and TSH — adrenal insufficiency mimics it perfectly and is fatal if missed.
- Volume status is unreliable; low uric acid (<4) and low BUN support SIADH vs hypovolemic (where both rise).
- Thiazides invalidate urine Na — exclude first. Urine osm <100 → primary polydipsia/beer potomania.
**Clinical reasoning** — Diagnostic schema, first split on volume status, then exclude mimics — guarding against premature closure ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q7 — Corticosteroids in septic shock
**Key trials** — [APROCCHSS (2018)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=APROCCHSS+hydrocortisone+fludrocortisone+septic+shock): hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone reduced 90-day mortality in pressor-dependent shock. [ADRENAL (2018)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ADRENAL+hydrocortisone+septic+shock): no mortality benefit but faster shock reversal. [SSC 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8486643/): weak recommendation to add in pressor-dependent shock.
**Teaching points**
- Trigger: norepinephrine ≥0.25 µg/kg/min for ≥4h after fluids — not every septic patient.
- Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day; no ACTH stim test.
- Trials differ by design: APROCCHSS enrolled sicker patients and added fludrocortisone; ADRENAL did not. Reproducible signal is hemodynamic, not necessarily mortality.
- Avoid in sepsis without shock; watch hyperglycemia/hypernatremia; wean as pressors come off.
**Clinical reasoning** — Surrogate-vs-outcome appraisal: faster shock reversal is consistent, mortality benefit population-dependent — match your patient to the sicker APROCCHSS phenotype ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q8 — Imaging acute low back pain
**Key trials** — Guideline consensus: ACP low back pain guideline ([ACP 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ACP+noninvasive+treatments+low+back+pain+guideline)) and ACP/APS imaging guidance ([2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ACP+diagnostic+imaging+low+back+pain)) — against routine early imaging absent red flags.
**Teaching points**
- Cauda equina is the can't-miss: urinary retention with overflow incontinence is the most sensitive early sign — emergency MRI, don't wait for the full triad.
- Suspected infection/malignancy: ESR/CRP + plain films a reasonable first screen; fever often absent in epidural abscess — use risk context (IVDU, immunosuppression, recent procedure).
- Classic error: imaging nonspecific pain/sciatica within 6 weeks — no outcome benefit, surfaces incidentalomas, drives procedures.
- Imaging only changes management if the patient is a surgical/injection candidate.
**Clinical reasoning** — Pretest-probability/Bayesian step: red flags raise the prior above the testing threshold, mirroring Wells/PERC; absent them, a positive scan is more likely a false-positive incidentaloma ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q9 — Hypertensive emergency vs urgency
**Key trials** — Guideline consensus: [ACC/AHA 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=2017+ACC+AHA+hypertension+guideline) defines the split by target-organ damage and the graded-lowering targets. No trial supports treating asymptomatic severe hypertension acutely.
**Teaching points**
- The number doesn't define the emergency — end-organ injury does. 230/130 asymptomatic with normal labs/ECG/fundi is *urgency*.
- Exceptions to "25% MAP" rule: aortic dissection (SBP 100–120 in minutes, β-blocker *first*), ischemic stroke (permissive; only lower >185/110 for tPA or >220/120).
- Classic error: sublingual/short-acting nifedipine or aggressive crash-lowering → watershed stroke, MI.
- Titratable IV agents (nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol) with arterial line.
**Clinical reasoning** — Competing-risk trade-off: sustained end-organ injury vs hypoperfusion from overcorrection, autoregulatory curve as the hinge; target-organ damage as the bifurcating problem-representation step ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).

## Q10 — Acute dialysis in AKI
**Key trials** — [AKIKI (2016)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=AKIKI+early+delayed+renal+replacement+therapy): early vs delayed RRT, no mortality difference; delayed avoided RRT in ~half. [STARRT-AKI (2020)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=STARRT-AKI+accelerated+renal+replacement): accelerated start no benefit, more dependence at 90d. ELAIN (single-center) suggested early benefit, overturned by the larger multicenter trials.
**Teaching points**
- "AEIOU" complications drive the decision — refractoriness to medical therapy, not an absolute number.
- Hyperkalemia with ECG changes or pH <7.1 are most time-sensitive; treat medically first, dialyze if refractory.
- Classic error: dialyzing a "scary" BUN/Cr in a stable, recovering patient — line sepsis and hemodynamic insults for no benefit.
- Modality follows hemodynamics: CRRT for shock, IHD if stable.
**Clinical reasoning** — Competing-risk trade-off: unnecessary line + RRT instability vs a delayed indication; pair with an AKI schema to avoid dialyzing a reversible pre-renal/obstructive cause ([Clinical Problem Solvers](https://clinicalproblemsolving.com)).
